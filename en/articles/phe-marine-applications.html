Plate Heat Exchanger Applications in the Marine Industry

📅 Jul 4, 2026 ⏱ 12 min read

Introduction

Plate Heat Exchangers (PHEs), valued for their high efficiency, compact footprint, and ease of disassembly, have become the most widely used heat exchange equipment on modern ships. From 100,000-ton ocean-going bulk carriers to cruise ships, from naval vessels to offshore platforms, nearly every modern vessel is equipped with multiple PHEs.

Ships impose unique demands on heat exchange equipment: seawater corrosion resistance, vibration tolerance in rolling environments, limited maintenance space, and extremely high safety and reliability standards. The modular design and high heat transfer efficiency of PHEs make them ideal for these challenging conditions.

This article provides a systematic overview of PHE applications across ship systems.

1. Central Cooling System (CCS)

1.1 System Overview

The Central Cooling System is the core auxiliary system for modern ship propulsion, and the Central Plate Cooler is its most critical heat exchange component. The system structure is as follows:

Low-Temp Freshwater Loop (closed)              Seawater Loop (open)
      │                                               │
      ├─→ Main engine jacket cooling                  │
      ├─→ Generator set cooling                       │
      ├─→ Air compressor cooling                      │
      ├─→ AC condenser cooling     ←──PHE──→     Overboard seawater (direct discharge)
      └─→ Other auxiliary equipment cooling           │

1.2 Technical Requirements for Central Plate Coolers

  • Large heat transfer area: Central coolers on large vessels typically have 200~800 m² of heat transfer area
  • Dual-pass design: Multi-pass on the freshwater side for higher efficiency; single-pass on the seawater side to minimize pressure drop
  • Seawater-resistant materials: Seawater-side plates typically use titanium (Ti Grade 1) or super stainless steel (SMO 254)
  • Anti-fouling protection: Used in conjunction with electrolytic anti-fouling systems (MGPS/ICAF)

2. Main Engine Lubricating Oil Cooling

2.1 Background

During normal operation, the lubricating oil temperature of low-speed two-stroke marine diesel engines must be maintained at 40~55°C. The lube oil cooler transfers heat from the circulating lubricating oil to the low-temperature freshwater, keeping the oil within its optimal operating temperature range.

2.2 Advantages of Plate-Type Lube Oil Coolers

Compared to traditional shell-and-tube coolers, PHE-based solutions offer:

  1. Higher heat transfer efficiency: PHEs achieve 3~5× the heat transfer coefficient of shell-and-tube units, requiring less surface area
  2. Ease of cleaning: Long-term lube oil usage produces sludge and carbon deposits — PHEs can be disassembled for plate-by-plate cleaning
  3. Space savings: Engine room space is extremely valuable; PHEs occupy only about 1/3 the volume of shell-and-tube units
  4. Scalability: Additional plates can be inserted to meet increased cooling demands from engine power upgrades

2.3 Selection Considerations

  • Lube oil viscosity is relatively high (typically ISO VG 30~50); select low-resistance herringbone corrugation patterns
  • Recommended plate material: 316L (freshwater side) / Titanium (seawater side)
  • Gasket material: NBR is the most common choice due to its excellent oil resistance

3. Main Engine Jacket Water Cooling

3.1 Working Principle

Jacket cooling water absorbs heat from cylinder walls and cylinder heads, reaching 80~85°C. It then passes through the jacket water cooler to be cooled to 65~70°C before re-entering the circulation loop.

Jacket water outlet (~85°C)  →  Jacket water cooler  →  Jacket water inlet (~68°C)
                                      ↑
                              LT freshwater (~36°C)

3.2 PHE in Jacket Water Cooling Applications

  • Hot side: Jacket cooling water (freshwater + anti-corrosion additive), 8085°C, PN6PN10
  • Cold side: Central low-temperature freshwater, 36~45°C

Due to high temperatures and large temperature differences, plates are prone to thermal stress and scale formation, requiring regular cleaning.

4. Fuel Oil Heating System

4.1 Why Fuel Heating Is Needed

Marine heavy fuel oil (HFO/IFO) at ambient temperature is extremely viscous (up to 700 cSt@50°C) and cannot be directly injected into the engine. It must be heated through fuel oil heaters to the following temperatures:

  • Settling tank heating: 50~60°C for oil-water separation and sediment settling
  • Service tank heating: 70~80°C to maintain fluidity
  • Pre-injection heating: 120140°C to reduce viscosity to 1015 cSt for proper atomization

4.2 Role of PHEs in Fuel Heating

Fuel heating systems typically involve multi-stage heat exchange:

Steam / thermal oil (heat source)
      │
      ▼
  Fuel heater (plate type)
      │
      ▼
  Fuel (HFO) → Heated to target temperature → Enters main engine

Selection considerations:

  • High-temperature operation (steam 150~180°C); gaskets must be EPDM or FKM (Viton)
  • HFO contains corrosive components (sulfur, vanadium); prioritize 316L or titanium plates
  • Plate corrugation should be anti-clogging design; deep corrugation + wide spacing recommended

5. HVAC and Refrigeration Systems

5.1 Uniqueness of Marine Air Conditioning

Marine air conditioning must serve multiple scenarios:

  • Accommodation HVAC: Comfortable living environment for crew/passengers
  • Engine control room HVAC: Temperature-controlled environment for automation equipment and personnel
  • Cargo hold refrigeration: Precise temperature control for reefer containers and cold storage

5.2 PHE Roles in Refrigeration Systems

In marine chiller systems, PHEs serve as both evaporators and condensers:

Role Media Plate Material Characteristics
Evaporator Refrigerant ↔ Chilled water 316L Refrigerant side requires pressure-resistant design
Condenser Refrigerant ↔ Cooling seawater Titanium / SMO254 Seawater side requires corrosion resistance

Brazed plate heat exchangers (BPHEs) are increasingly used in marine refrigeration for their zero-leakage risk and gasket-free design. Common brands include SWEP and Alfa Laval CB Series.

6. Freshwater Generators

6.1 Working Principle

Shipboard freshwater generators use waste heat from main engine jacket water as a heat source to evaporate seawater and condense the vapor into fresh water:

ME jacket water (heat source, ~80°C)
      │
      ▼
  Plate preheater → Plate evaporator → Plate condenser
      │                   │                  │
      ▼                   ▼                  ▼
  Seawater preheated   Seawater evaporates   Steam condenses into fresh water

6.2 Advantages of PHEs

  • Energy efficient: Utilizes waste heat with virtually no additional fuel consumption
  • Compact design: Small overall footprint suited for engine room installation
  • Corrosion resistant: Titanium plates on the seawater side ensure long service life
  • High output: A single plate-type freshwater generator can produce 20~50 tons of fresh water per day

Alfa Laval's AQUA Series and JWP Series are market leaders in marine freshwater generators.

7. Ballast Water Treatment Systems

7.1 Background

Under the IMO Ballast Water Management Convention (BWM Convention), all internationally sailing vessels must be equipped with a Ballast Water Management System (BWMS), typically incorporating filtration + UV or filtration + electrolysis stages.

7.2 PHE Applications

In electrolysis-based BWMS, active substances generated by electrolysis (sodium hypochlorite, etc.) must be neutralized with seawater. PHEs are used in this process for:

  • Electrolyte cooling: Maintaining electrolyzer temperature within optimal range (20~30°C)
  • Neutralization heat exchange: Adjusting temperature and chemical parameters of neutralized water

Due to the highly corrosive nature of seawater media, PHEs in this application must use titanium plates or duplex stainless steel.

8. Other Auxiliary Systems

8.1 Exhaust Gas Economizer Heat Exchange

Main engine exhaust temperatures reach 300400°C. Exhaust gas economizers recover this waste heat to generate steam. Feedwater preheaters typically use PHEs, improving overall system thermal efficiency by 35%.

8.2 Hydraulic System Cooling

Hydraulic oil for steering gear, windlasses, cranes, and other deck machinery must be cooled via plate coolers. Since hydraulic systems require precise temperature control (typically 40~60°C), the fast response characteristics of PHEs are particularly advantageous.

8.3 Scrubber Cooling

Since the implementation of IMO 2020 sulfur cap, many vessels have installed exhaust gas cleaning systems (scrubbers). The scrubber wash water requires cooling circulation, and PHEs are the standard configuration for this system.

9.2 Design Margins

A 15~20% surplus in heat transfer area is recommended to account for:

  • Gradual seawater-side fouling reducing heat transfer capacity
  • Potential engine power upgrades during the vessel's lifetime
  • Extreme operating conditions (tropical seawater temperatures up to 32°C)

9.3 Classification Society Approvals

Marine heat exchange equipment typically requires certification from major classification societies:

  • CCS — China Classification Society
  • DNV GL — DNV GL (Norway/Germany)
  • Lloyd's Register — UK
  • ABS — American Bureau of Shipping
  • BV — Bureau Veritas (France)

Leading brands including Alfa Laval, GEA, and Tranter hold approvals from all major classification societies.

9.4 Spare Parts and Maintenance Considerations

  • Keep at least 1 set of gaskets and 3~5 spare plates on board for emergency repairs
  • Choose brands with global port service networks (e.g., Alfa Laval, GEA) for support during voyages
  • Prioritize glue-free gasket systems (ClipGrip™, etc.) to reduce crew replacement difficulty

Conclusion

Plate heat exchangers serve virtually every heat exchange need on modern ships — from core propulsion cooling to crew accommodation heating and hot water. As IMO environmental regulations become increasingly stringent and ship automation continues to advance, PHE applications in waste heat recovery, emissions treatment, and energy efficiency will continue to expand.

For shipowners and technical managers, understanding PHE application scenarios and selection criteria across ship systems enables more economical, reliable equipment configuration and maintenance decisions.
